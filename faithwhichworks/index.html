Faith Which Works

A Study Guide to Righteousness by Faith Theology

Overview: Righteousness by Faith

This study explores the theological contributions of two key figures in Seventh-day Adventist theology who championed the doctrine of righteousness by faith as an antidote to legalism.

The Central Problem

Traditional views of justification can create a "divorce" between legal standing and internal transformation, leading to legalistic anxiety and focus on human performance rather than divine grace.

The Solution

Both theologians sought to unite justification and sanctification into a single, transformative experience where righteousness is not merely declared but imparted, creating genuine heart change that naturally produces righteous living.

Historical Context

The 1888 General Conference session in Minneapolis marked a pivotal moment where these principles were presented to the Seventh-day Adventist Church, though their full acceptance and implementation remained a matter of ongoing discussion.

E.J. Waggoner (1855-1916)

Prominent physician, theologian, and minister who championed righteousness by faith at the historic 1888 General Conference session in Minneapolis. Waggoner's theology offered a solution to legalism by presenting justification as a transformative, imparted reality.
Core Theological Concepts

Righteousness as "Right-Doing"

Waggoner defined righteousness simply as right-doing, with God's law serving as the standard. The law represents not arbitrary rules but a perfect transcript of God's character.

Christ as the Living Law

Jesus Christ is the law in living form - the "express image of God's person." His life perfectly demonstrates the law's principles, making Him both the standard and the means of righteousness.

The New Covenant Mechanism

Through the New Covenant, believers are not just declared righteous but made righteous. Christ's literal indwelling presence writes the law on the heart, fulfilling Jeremiah 31:33 and Hebrews 8:10.

Faith as Reception

Faith is not a meritorious work but simply the act of receiving. It is the hand that accepts the gift of Christ Himself. Since Christ is our righteousness, receiving Him means receiving righteousness.

Key Works
Christ and His Righteousness (1890) Foundational work defining righteousness and explaining how it is imparted through Christ.
The Glad Tidings (1900) Commentary on Galatians presenting a thorough refutation of legalism.
The Everlasting Covenant (1900) Argues for the unity of the covenant of grace throughout Scripture.
Studies in Romans Detailed exposition on the power of the gospel as presented in Romans.

Robert J. Wieland (1916-2002)

20th-century pastor, missionary, and author who partnered with Donald K. Short to advocate for a revival of the 1888 message. Wieland framed righteousness by faith as a deeply relational experience centered on heart appreciation for God's agape love.
Theological Framework

The "Unaccepted" 1888 Message

Wieland argued that the 1888 message was largely misunderstood and resisted, resulting in spiritual "wilderness wandering" for the denomination. His work called for a return to this message as the key to revival.

Faith as Divine Gift and Gratitude

True faith is not human achievement but a divine gift - specifically, a "heart appreciation" for God's love. This gratitude naturally expresses itself in good works, removing the anxiety of performance-based religion.

The Revelation of Agape

The catalyst for transformative faith is understanding God's selfless, unconditional love (agape). This love is fundamentally different from human love and serves as the worthy object of our appreciation.

The Cross and the "Second Death"

Christ's ultimate demonstration of agape was experiencing the "second death" - complete separation from the Father - without visible hope of resurrection. This supreme sacrifice inspires faith that works by love.

Key Works
1888 Re-Examined (with D.K. Short) Historical and theological argument for the significance of the 1888 message.
Grace on Trial Demonstrates the full power of grace to overcome sin, not just pardon it.
In Search of the Cross Explores Christ's experience of the "second death" as catalyst for faith.
Gold Tried in the Fire Connects the 1888 message to the Laodicean message of Revelation 3.

Theological Comparison

E.J. Waggoner

Focus

Ontological transformation through Christ's indwelling

Key Mechanism

New Covenant "heart surgery" - law written on the heart

Definition of Faith

Reception - the hand that accepts Christ

Approach

Systematic theology emphasizing union with Christ

Robert J. Wieland

Focus

Relational transformation through appreciation of agape

Key Mechanism

Heart appreciation sparked by revelation of God's love

Definition of Faith

Gratitude - divine gift of heart appreciation

Approach

Motivational emphasis on the Cross and divine love

Common Ground

Shared Principles

  • Unity of justification and sanctification
  • Righteousness as imparted, not just imputed
  • Faith as divine gift, not human work
  • Transformation from within, not external conformity
  • Antidote to legalism through grace-based motivation
  • Emphasis on the 1888 message's significance
